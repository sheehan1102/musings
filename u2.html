U2 Discography

In this musing, I'm going to listen to every U2 album in full, sequentially, the initial released version and come up with: 1) Their best album, 2) An Honorable Mention (or two), 3) Their worst album, 4) The top five songs. Let's begin!

Boy (1980)

I Will Follow: relentless Edge power chords, tingle tanlge of '80s bell synth, Bono's pounding vocals, with the boys coming in on "walk away", just smacks of driving Dublin club music and already hearing their sound.

Twilight: Now we have the sex monster Adam Clayton with a heavy bass line playing lead, Bono's voice synthing into the mist. It's got that dark, Irish pop, like you can hear the pain and the grind to rise above it all. The chord progression is unexpected, odd when we're used to pop cadence which doesn't vear off the main path very often. Edge with a dim club guitar solo, the type of thing they'll move away from in future albums.

An Cat Dubh: strange kick-off, but Bono's melody is gorgeous. Clayton leads us out, but we get into some heavy rock chords and more bells. They fucking love the bells. Slow pace to this one, but it's weirdly catchy. Not quite a ballad, not quite rock 'n' roll, no home for this one commercially, but gives a good acid trip vibe. The anchor is Bono's melody which focuses the beauty at the center.

Into The Heart: This is a classic deep cut: strange intro, poetic lyrics, probably hyper-Christian, fucking bells; I'm here for it.

Out Of Control: Welcome back from that trip to subtlety. We're back in the club, the post-punk dawn of a new era. We have harmony with Edge, driving power on the guitar, Larry Mullins Jr. setting the pace with the classic U2 snare, simply rhythms done well, bridge flourishes to get me in the mood, Bono trying to fuck everyone in the audience, crisp Edge crescendos during the calmer sections. The try to whip you into a frenzy, and it comes off with pretty good effect.

Stories For Boys: Hello Mr. Mullins with a pounding intro, the type we'll get used to. Strange melody from Bono, another U2 hallmark. It's the way Mullins and Edge speak to one another that wins the day here. Give me more of that shit Larry! Oh, and we get more bells. They loved the fucking bells in the early days. Who played the bell when they were gigging in Dublin? Was Larry touching it in between motherfucking those drums? Another staple arriving here: Edge singing a background melody while he shreds. He has a unique voice and it adds dimension.

The Ocean: A bit of mysticism, slow pace, water sounds in the background. They weren't gigging with this one. Even more of a deep cut that Into The Heart. What the fuck just happened?

A Day Without Me: Getting a nice loopy bassy subtone for Bono to croon over. He does it well, then steps aside for gentle Edge electric. No face melting, just crisp, synthy fun. Crisp isn't the right word, because it all melts, but it's executed like Edge is in control of his craft. No complaints, that's a good tune.

Another Time Another Place: Everyone's here, including Clayton drolling a bass line, Larry bouncing, Edge pouncing at the edges, Bono sexing us up in his lower registers. He almost talks at times, like he's an ancient bard speaking in the abstract about a forgotten lover, or God, or Ireland. The title is a bit ironic, since they are so purely Irish and grungy and grinding and infused with the stale beer smell and a hope for something more.

The Electric Co.: This is what we've been waiting for. Lightening pace on the Edge leadout, Larry pounding into our hearts and souls, Bono's melody is percussive, the vocals are all over the map, but this song is a singular focus. This is the one, this is why they made Boy, so they could inject this into our fucking veins. Edge is so fucking fast, the softening into the harding gets the blood pumping. All the members are afterthoughts in this, because they are cohesive and I'm high as a kite. It's the most realized song of the album.

Shadows And Tall Trees/Saturday Matinee: here's the sign-off, with slow Bono melody, almost folksy drums, strange poetic lyrics, Edge harmony, slow bass ramblings, and Larry basically in handcuffs, trying to snatch a beat here or there. The long farewell to a solid album. The finish is catchy, the shouted "shadow" in triplicate remdinds me of something, like it's the most distilled five seconds of U2 in existence.

Big Picture: this album is a relic, no doubt. It's putty that's almost shaped into a band, glimpses of beauty and power and sex and poetry. Each of the component members has a memorable moment or three. When would you put this record on for a listen? Maybe after a night out in the grunge of a city, an afternoon contemplating the rain, post-shower before heading to the pub. It's good, it's not brilliant, Electric Co. is an absolute powerhouse, and had to have gotten them a contract for album #2. It's a B.

Post mortem: Produced by Steve Lillywhite, apparently Larry Mullins Jr. recorded much of his drumming in a stairwell (!) giving it that crashing, pub sound. Was not hyper successful, though it got them out on the road on the continent and in the states. I'll take credit for calling them "putty", since they still didn't really know how to play together, and Bono wasn't much for set lyrics. The "post-punk" reference holds water, as the timing and sound work, and critics have called it the same. Wonderful start to the U2 musing.
